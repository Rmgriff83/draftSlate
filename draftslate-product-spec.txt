1. PRODUCT OVERVIEW
================================================================================

DraftSlate is a competitive fantasy-style sports platform where users manage
a "team" made up of sportsbook-style slate picks rather than real
athletes. Think of it as fantasy football, except instead of drafting Patrick
Mahomes or Travis Kelce, you are drafting specific slate picks — for
example: "Rashee Rice will record 7 or more receptions against the Bears on
Sunday."

Each week, league members participate in a live draft to select their slate for
the upcoming slate of games. Those picks are assigned to either their starting
lineup or bench. At the end of the week, each user is matched head-to-head
against another league member, and whoever's starting slate produces more
correct slate picks wins that matchup — exactly like a fantasy football win/loss
record.

At the end of the season, the user with the best win/loss record (or the winner
of a playoff bracket, depending on league settings) wins the league pot. The
platform takes a 10% commission from the total pot; the rest is distributed to
the winner(s) per the commissioner's configured payout structure.

The MVP focuses exclusively on NFL. All slate picks are NFL-based player
props and game slate picks pulled from the-odds-api.com.


================================================================================
2. CORE CONCEPT & GAMEPLAY PHILOSOPHY
================================================================================

The central idea bridges two popular but distinct worlds:

  - Fantasy Sports: season-long competition, weekly matchups, head-to-head
    records, rosters, drafts, standings, and playoffs.

  - Sports Betting / slate Apps: outcome-based predictions with real odds,
    over/under player props, spread and moneyline picks.

What makes DraftSlate unique is the combination of:

  a) A live weekly draft where users compete to claim the best available slate picks
     before their opponents do — introducing strategy and scarcity.

  b) A head-to-head matchup format with win/loss records, standings, and
     playoffs — giving the season meaning beyond a single week.

  c) An odds floor system that ensures picks are not trivially safe — users
     cannot stack their slate with -800 near-certainties. By default, every
     pick must meet a global minimum odds threshold (default: -250, meaning
     no single pick can be more than roughly a 71% implied probability
     favorite). This floor is commissioner-configurable per league. Leagues
     can also go further and enforce per-slot odds targets, where each
     starting slot has a specific required odds band rather than a single
     floor — see Section 5.4 for full details.

  d) Active slate management throughout the week — odds shift after the draft,
     games get closer, injury news breaks. Users must swap bench slate into
     their starting slate strategically before game time locks their selections.

  e) A social, commissioner-led league experience with private groups of friends
     or public competitive leagues.

The platform is designed to feel familiar to fantasy sports players while
layering in the excitement and strategy of odds-based predictions.

3. USER ROLES & ACCOUNT TYPES
================================================================================

--- 3.1 Guest / Unauthenticated User ---

Can browse the marketing site and see public league leaderboards (read-only).
Cannot participate in any league activity or draft. Must register to play.

--- 3.2 Registered User (Free Tier) ---

A standard registered account. Can:
  - Join public or private leagues (via invite code for private leagues)
  - Participate in drafts for any league they belong to
  - Manage their slate week-to-week
  - Customize their team name, team logo, and profile avatar
  - View their personal stats, history, and pick accuracy over time
  - Create up to 1 public league AND 1 private league as commissioner (free)

--- 3.3 Commissioner ---

Any registered user who creates a league becomes the commissioner of that
league. The commissioner has elevated permissions within their league:

  - Set league name, size (number of teams), and league type (public/private)
  - Configure slate size (total picks per roster, default: 8 — 5 starters + 3 bench)
  - Configure minimum aggregate odds threshold for draft selections
  - Set the buy-in amount per team (how much each member contributes to the pot)
  - Set payout structure (e.g., 100% to 1st, or 90% to 1st / 10% to 2nd, etc.)
  - Set draft format settings (draft window duration, randomization behavior)
  - Invite members to private leagues via a shareable invite code
  - Remove members from a league before the season starts
  - Cannot change buy-in or payout settings after the season begins

Free-tier commissioners can manage 1 public + 1 private league simultaneously.
Subscribed commissioners can create and manage unlimited leagues.

--- 3.4 Platform Admin ---

Internal staff role. Can manage all leagues, users, payouts, and platform
settings. Not part of the user-facing product — admin panel only.


================================================================================
4. LEAGUE SYSTEM
================================================================================

--- 4.1 League Types ---

PUBLIC LEAGUE: Listed in a league browser. Any registered user can join up to
the league's maximum team count. No invite code required.

PRIVATE LEAGUE: Not listed publicly. Users can only join with a commissioner-
issued invite code or direct link.

--- 4.2 League Settings (Configured at Creation) ---

  - League name
  - League type: public or private
  - Maximum number of teams (e.g., 8, 10, 12, 14 — must be even for matchups)
  - Buy-in amount per team (in USD, minimum $5)
  - Payout structure (configurable percentage splits across 1st, 2nd, etc.)
  - Total slate size (default: 8 — 5 starters + 3 bench)
  - Odds enforcement mode: Global Minimum Floor (default) or Per-Slot Odds Bands
      - If Global Floor: configurable minimum odds value (default: -250)
      - If Per-Slot Bands: per-slot min/max odds pairs for each starting slot,
        plus optional bench floor
  - Weekly draft day (default: Tuesday) and draft start time
  - Draft pick timer duration (how long each user has per slate pick; default: 60s)
  - Season length (number of weeks, default: follows NFL regular season weeks)
  - Playoff format (see Section 8 for full playoff configuration options)

Settings are locked once the season begins (after the first draft concludes).
The commissioner may update league name and invite settings at any time.

--- 4.3 League States ---

PENDING: League created, not yet full. Members can join. Season has not started.

ACTIVE: Season is in progress. Weekly drafts, matchups, and slate management
are occurring.

PLAYOFFS: If enabled, the top N teams by win/loss record enter a single-
elimination playoff bracket at the end of the regular season.

COMPLETED: Season finished. Payouts have been distributed. League is archived
and read-only.

CANCELLED: Commissioner cancelled the league before it started. All collected
buy-ins are fully refunded to members.

--- 4.4 Joining a League ---

Users browsing public leagues see: league name, commissioner, current/max team
count, buy-in amount, and the week the season starts. They click "Join" and are
prompted to pay their buy-in before their spot is reserved.

For private leagues, users receive a shareable invite link or alphanumeric code.
They still pay the buy-in during the join flow.

A user may not join a league after the season has begun (Week 1 draft has
started or concluded).

--- 4.5 Buy-In Payment & Trust Holding ---

When a user joins a league, they pay the configured buy-in amount through the
platform's payment processor (Stripe). The funds are held in the platform's
trust account — they are not released to any winner until the season concludes
and a champion is determined.

If a league is cancelled before the season begins, all buy-ins are refunded in
full. Refund logic is automated via a background job.

--- 4.6 Team Identity ---

Each user in a league has a "team" associated with that league. Team fields:
  - Team name (user-customizable, profanity filtered)
  - Team logo (user-uploaded image or selected from a preset library)
  - Owner (the user account)

Users can have different team identities in each league they belong to.

5. THE DRAFT
================================================================================

--- 5.1 What Gets Drafted ---

The available slate pool each week is a curated set of NFL slate picks
pulled from the-odds-api.com. These are player props and game outcome
predictions tied to the upcoming week's NFL games.

Examples of draft selections:
  - "Patrick Mahomes passes for 280+ yards vs. the Raiders" (odds: -160)
  - "Ja'Marr Chase records 6+ receptions vs. the Steelers" (odds: +110)
  - "Bills win by 7+ points over the Jets" (odds: -130)
  - "Saquon Barkley rushes for 80+ yards vs. the Cowboys" (odds: -200)

Each draft selection has:
  - A plain-English description of the outcome
  - The sport (NFL in MVP)
  - The game it belongs to (team matchup + date/time)
  - Current American odds at the time of slate pool creation
  - A lock time (when the game starts — picks lock before kickoff)

--- 5.2 Slate Pool Curation & Timing ---

The slate pool is assembled immediately before the draft window opens each week.
A background job fires automatically at the scheduled draft start time (or just
prior — see below), fetches the latest available NFL odds from the-odds-api.com,
filters eligible picks, and makes the pool live to all league members. This
ensures that the odds snapshot used for the draft reflects the most current
available market data rather than data that may be hours or days stale.

DRAFT TIMING: The default draft day is Tuesday night (configurable per league
by the commissioner — see Section 4.2). Tuesday is the ideal default because:
  - All Sunday and Monday Night Football games from the prior week have
    concluded, keeping users engaged with fresh results
  - The upcoming week's game lines and player props are freshly posted by
    bookmakers following the prior week's final game
  - It gives users the full week (Wednesday through the weekend) for slate management
    management before Sunday games kick off

The commissioner may shift the draft day/time to any day and time of their
choosing when creating the league. Common alternatives include Wednesday night
or Thursday afternoon (before Thursday Night Football kicks off).

DRAFT POOL BUILD JOB TIMING: The SLATE_POOL_BUILD_JOB fires automatically
approximately 15–30 minutes before the scheduled draft start time. This short
pre-draft window allows the job to complete and the pool to be ready for users
when the draft lobby opens, while ensuring the odds are as fresh as possible.
The draft itself does not start until the job has completed successfully —
if the job fails or times out, a retry is triggered and a brief draft delay
notification is sent to all league members.

CRITICAL DESIGN DECISION: The slate pool is intentionally built from picks
whose games are far enough in the future that odds movement during the 3–4 hour
draft window will be minimal. For the NFL on a Tuesday night draft, this means:

  - The current week's Sunday and Monday Night games are the primary pick
    source — their kickoffs are 4–6 days away, so odds are reasonably stable
    during the draft window
  - Thursday Night Football picks for the current week are EXCLUDED from the
    slate pool if the Thursday game is within 24 hours of the draft time,
    since odds for imminent games move rapidly and the draft window could
    overlap with or run close to kickoff
  - Thursday Night Football picks for a Thursday game that is more than 24
    hours away (e.g., a commissioner has set a Monday or early-Wednesday
    draft) may be included at the platform's discretion — configurable via
    platform settings

The platform uses snapshot odds (frozen at slate pool creation time, i.e., the
moment the SLATE_POOL_BUILD_JOB runs) for purposes of verifying the minimum
odds threshold — not live odds at the moment of each user's individual pick
selection during the draft.

--- 5.3 Draft Format ---

The draft is a SNAKE DRAFT format. Draft order each week is determined by a
weighted randomization system designed to provide competitive parity — teams
that performed worse the prior week are given a higher probability of drawing
an earlier pick, without the outcome being fully deterministic.

WEIGHTED DRAFT ORDER SYSTEM:

  At the start of each week's draft, the system calculates a draft order weight
  for every team in the league based on their correct pick count from the
  previous week's starting slate. Teams with fewer correct slate picks receive a
  higher weight (greater probability of drawing an early position); teams with
  more correct slate picks receive a lower weight.

  The weighting formula assigns each team a weight inversely proportional to
  their prior week's correct pick count. A simple implementation:

    weight = (max_possible_correct_picks - prior_week_correct_picks) + 1

  For example, in a league with 5 starting slots (max 5 correct slate picks):
    Team with 0 correct slate picks last week → weight of 6  (highest chance)
    Team with 1 correct pick last week  → weight of 5
    Team with 2 correct slate picks last week → weight of 4
    Team with 3 correct slate picks last week → weight of 3
    Team with 4 correct slate picks last week → weight of 2
    Team with 5 correct slate picks last week → weight of 1  (lowest chance)

  Draft positions are then drawn sequentially — position 1 first, then
  position 2, and so on — using weighted random selection without replacement.
  A team already assigned a position is removed from the pool before the next
  position is drawn.

  This means a team that went 0-for-5 last week is six times more likely to
  land the first pick than a team that went 5-for-5 — but the top performer
  still has a non-zero chance of drawing an early position, preserving
  unpredictability and preventing the draft order from being fully anticipated
  or strategized around.

WEEK 1 EXCEPTION:
  There is no prior week data for the season's first draft. Week 1 draft order
  is determined by pure random draw (all teams weighted equally).

SNAKE FORMAT:
  Once positions are assigned via weighted draw, the draft proceeds in standard
  snake format: Round 1 goes position 1 → N, Round 2 goes N → 1, Round 3 goes
  1 → N, and so on. In a 10-team league with 8 slate spots, there are 8 rounds.

DRAFT ORDER DISPLAY:
  The weighted draw results are published to all league members when the draft
  lobby opens (i.e., after the SLATE_POOL_BUILD_JOB completes and the draft
  enters the ready state). Members can see the full pick order before the draft
  begins. The prior week scores that produced the weighting are also displayed
  for transparency.

PLAYOFF DRAFT ORDER:
  During playoff weeks, only the teams still active in the bracket participate
  in that week's draft. Weighting is applied the same way using the prior
  week's playoff matchup scores. Teams eliminated from the winners bracket but
  still playing in the losers bracket are included in their bracket's draft.

--- 5.4 Minimum Odds Threshold & Per-Slot Odds Configuration ---

The platform supports two distinct odds enforcement modes, configured per league
by the commissioner at league creation. These are mutually exclusive — a league
uses one mode or the other, not both simultaneously.

MODE 1 — GLOBAL MINIMUM ODDS FLOOR (default)

  Every pick a user selects during the draft must have odds AT or ABOVE the
  league's configured minimum threshold at the time the slate pool snapshot
  was taken. The default threshold is -250 American odds.

  Understanding the threshold direction:
    - A pick with odds of -200 is ALLOWED (less favored than -250 = more risk)
    - A pick with odds of -300 is NOT ALLOWED (more favored than -250 = too safe)
    - A pick with odds of +150 is ALLOWED (underdog = easily clears the floor)
    - A pick with odds of -250 is ALLOWED (exactly meets the floor)

  The threshold is global and per-pick — each individual pick must independently
  clear the floor. There is no averaging or aggregate loophole. The commissioner
  can set this value to any American odds value when configuring the league.
  Common examples:

    -150   tight floor — only strong favorites are restricted
    -250   default — moderate favorites allowed, heavy favorites blocked
    -400   loose floor — most picks qualify; only near-certainties blocked
    +100   strict floor — only even-money or underdog picks allowed (very
           aggressive league setting for high-variance gameplay)

  The system enforces this at the UI level (ineligible picks are grayed out or
  hidden during the draft) and at the server level upon pick submission.

MODE 2 — PER-SLOT ODDS BANDS (advanced, commissioner-configured)

  Instead of a single global floor, the commissioner defines an explicit odds
  target for each starting slate slot. Each slot has a required odds band —
  a range of American odds within which the slate pick drafted into that slot must
  fall (using snapshot odds at slate pool creation time). Bench slots are not
  subject to slot odds requirements and only need to clear a configurable
  bench minimum floor (default: no restriction on bench slate in this mode).

  This mode creates deliberate strategic layers in the roster. Users must draft
  one slate pick per required odds band, introducing portfolio-style construction
  rather than a free-for-all within a single floor.

  EXAMPLE CONFIGURATION (5 starting slots):
    Slot 1 (High Risk):    +100 or better (even money or underdog)
    Slot 2 (Medium Risk):  Between -100 and +99
    Slot 3 (Moderate):     Between -250 and -101
    Slot 4 (Lower Risk):   Between -500 and -251
    Slot 5 (Safe Anchor):  -501 or worse (heavy favorite anchor pick)

  Each slot band is defined as a min and max American odds value. A pick is
  eligible for a given slot if its snapshot odds fall within that band. During
  the draft, users select which slot they are drafting a slate pick into (or the UI
  guides them with available slot options per slate pick). A slate pick can only fill one
  slot, and each slot can only hold one slate pick.

  HOW BANDS ARE EXPRESSED IN SETTINGS:
  The commissioner defines slot bands as an ordered list of (min_odds, max_odds)
  pairs — one pair per starting slot. The number of pairs must equal the
  configured number of starting slots. Bench slots inherit a separate optional
  bench floor (defaults to unrestricted in this mode).

  DRAFT BEHAVIOR IN MODE 2:
    - The available slate pick list during the draft shows which slots each slate pick is
      eligible for based on its odds band
    - A user selects a slate pick and the slot they want to place it in; the system
      validates eligibility server-side
    - Auto-pick (on timer expiry) selects the highest-odds pick available for
      the user's next unfilled slot, in slot order (Slot 1 first)
    - If no eligible pick exists for a required slot (pool exhausted for that
      band), the slot remains empty and scores 0 for the week — this is an
      edge case the commissioner should guard against by ensuring the draft
      pool is large enough relative to league size and slot band widths

  COMMISSIONER UI FOR MODE 2:
  The league settings screen presents a slot configuration table where the
  commissioner sets the number of starting slots and defines the odds band
  for each slot. The UI validates that bands do not overlap and that the
  overall structure is sensible (e.g., warns if a band is so narrow that
  few picks will ever qualify for it given typical NFL odds ranges).

BENCH PICKS (BOTH MODES):
  Bench picks in Mode 1 must also clear the global minimum floor.
  Bench picks in Mode 2 are subject to the separate bench floor only
  (default: no restriction). This allows users to hold speculative high-
  upside bench slate as potential swap-ins without odds constraints.

--- 5.5 Draft Interface ---

The draft screen is the most time-sensitive and interactive screen in the app.
It must be fast, mobile-optimized, and clear. Key UI elements:

  - A countdown timer prominently displayed showing seconds remaining for the
    current pick
  - The current drafter's name/team highlighted clearly
  - A sortable/filterable list of available slate picks (by game, by odds, by player,
    by position/category)
  - Each pick shows: description, game, game time, odds, and eligibility status
  - A "your roster" panel showing what the current user has already selected
    and remaining empty slots
  - All teams' current roster builds visible (opponents can see what's been
    drafted, just like fantasy)
  - Auto-pick behavior is clearly communicated via a notification or countdown
    warning when a user's time is almost expired

--- 5.6 Draft Scheduling ---

The commissioner sets a draft day and time when creating the league. The
platform default is Tuesday night (e.g., 8:00 PM local time or a platform-
configured default time zone). This default is editable — the commissioner
can choose any day and time that works for their league.

Users receive reminders via push notification and/or email in advance of the
draft (e.g., 24 hours before, 1 hour before, 15 minutes before). The draft
begins at the scheduled time regardless of whether all users are present —
absent users are served by the auto-pick system.

The SLATE_POOL_BUILD_JOB fires automatically 15–30 minutes before the
scheduled draft start time (see Section 5.2). The draft lobby shows a
"preparing slate pool" state during this window, then transitions to "draft
live" once the job completes. If the build job is still running when the
scheduled start time arrives, the draft is held in the lobby state until
the job completes (with a visible status indicator for users), then starts
immediately upon completion.


================================================================================
6. SLATE MANAGEMENT
================================================================================

--- 6.1 Roster Structure ---

After the draft, each user has a full slate for that week. By default:

  - 5 STARTING SLATE slots (these picks count toward the matchup score)
  - 3 BENCH slots (held in reserve; can be swapped into the starting slate)
  - 8 TOTAL slate spots (configurable per league by commissioner)

The starting slate is what determines win/loss for the week. Bench picks do
not count toward the score — they are available as swaps.

--- 6.2 Slate Swapping (Active Management) ---

Between the conclusion of the draft and the lock time of each game, users can
swap picks between their starting slate and bench. This is the core
"management" skill layer of the game:

  - Users may move a bench pick to a starting slot (and vice versa) at any time
    before the relevant game's lock time (kickoff)
  - Once a game's kickoff time passes, the slate picks for that game are LOCKED in
    their current position (starter or bench) and cannot be moved
  - A user might move a slate pick to the bench if they see injury news or a line
    movement that makes a slate pick less confident
  - A user might promote a bench pick if they see favorable conditions develop
    (e.g., key defensive player ruled out for the opponent)

--- 6.3 Slate Lock Logic ---

Each pick is tied to a specific NFL game. The pick's lock time equals the
game's scheduled kickoff time. The system enforces this via server-side
validation — client-side timers are for UX guidance only and are not trusted
for lock enforcement.

If a slate pick is in the starting slate at its lock time, it is permanently locked
as a starter for that week. If it is on the bench at lock time, it is
permanently locked as a bench pick and will not count toward the matchup score
regardless of its outcome.

--- 6.4 Odds Display During Slate Management ---

After the draft, the app displays LIVE odds from the-odds-api.com alongside
each drafted pick. This allows users to see how odds have moved since the draft.

IMPORTANT: Live odds are for informational/UX purposes only. The outcome of a
pick is binary — it either hit or it didn't — regardless of odds movement.
Odds movement is a signal for the user to reconsider a slate pick's value, but it
does not change the scoring of the slate pick.

The platform fetches updated odds on a scheduled basis (e.g., every few hours)
to keep the displayed odds reasonably current without burning through API quota.
See Section 11 and Section 17 for API rate limit handling.


================================================================================
7. WEEKLY MATCHUP SCORING
================================================================================

--- 7.1 Head-to-Head Format ---

Each week, every team in the league is randomly matched against exactly one
other team (with no repeated matchup in the same regular season week, where
possible). This follows the standard fantasy football matchup model.

--- 7.2 Scoring ---

Scoring is simple and binary:

  - Each pick in a user's STARTING SLATE that HITS (the outcome occurs) = 1 point
  - Each pick that MISSES (the outcome does not occur) = 0 points
  - Pushes (if applicable, e.g., a game is postponed or a slate pick becomes void) =
    0 points (not counted for or against)

The user with more points at the end of the week's game slate wins the matchup.

TIEBREAKER: If both users have the same number of correct slate picks, the matchup is
recorded as a TIE. Tie-breaking for playoff seeding uses head-to-head record,
then total correct slate picks over the season, then coin flip (randomized server-side
and logged for auditing).

--- 7.3 Result Determination ---

Once a game concludes, the platform's background job system processes the game
results:

  1. Fetches final game scores and player stat lines from the data source
  2. Evaluates each drafted pick's outcome (hit or miss) based on the final stats
  3. Updates each slate pick's status in the database
  4. Calculates weekly scores for each team
  5. Determines the week's matchup winner(s)
  6. Updates league standings (win/loss records)
  7. Sends push notifications to users with their results

This processing happens via queued background jobs (see Section 15) to handle
the load of potentially many leagues and picks resolving simultaneously on
Sunday nights and Monday nights.

--- 7.4 Standings ---

League standings display:
  - Rank
  - Team name
  - Win / Loss / Tie record
  - Total correct slate picks (season aggregate — used as tiebreaker)
  - Points For / Points Against (total weekly pick-hit counts)
  - Last week's result


================================================================================
8. SEASON STRUCTURE & CHAMPIONSHIP
================================================================================

--- 8.1 Regular Season ---

The regular season follows the NFL schedule. Commissioners choose how many
weeks to designate as the regular season (e.g., Weeks 1–13 of the NFL season),
leaving the remaining weeks for the playoff bracket. The exact week count is
configurable at league creation.

--- 8.2 Playoff Structure Overview ---

Playoffs are enabled by default and are highly configurable. The playoff system
mirrors the structure used in established fantasy sports platforms, supporting
both a winners bracket (for the top teams) and a losers bracket (for the middle
and bottom teams), so every league member has meaningful games to play through
the end of the season — not just the top performers.

The commissioner configures the playoff format when creating the league. Once
the regular season ends, playoff seeding is determined by win/loss record with
tiebreakers applied (see Section 7.2), and the bracket is automatically
generated and locked.

--- 8.3 Playoff Bracket Configurations ---

The platform supports the following configurable playoff formats. The
commissioner selects one when setting up the league:

FORMAT A — TOP 4, WINNERS BRACKET ONLY (2 playoff weeks)
  For leagues of 8–10 teams.
  Week 1 (Semifinals): Seed 1 vs Seed 4 | Seed 2 vs Seed 3
  Week 2 (Championship): Semifinal winners play for 1st place
  No losers bracket. Seeds 5+ finish the season at their regular season
  standing with no playoff games.

FORMAT B — TOP 4 WITH CONSOLATION (2 playoff weeks)
  For leagues of 8–10 teams.
  Week 1 (Semifinals): Seed 1 vs Seed 4 | Seed 2 vs Seed 3
  Week 2: Championship game (semifinal winners) + 3rd place game (losers)
  Determines official 1st, 2nd, and 3rd place finishes.
  Seeds 5+ finish at regular season standing.

FORMAT C — TOP 6, WINNERS + LOSERS BRACKETS (3 playoff weeks)
  For leagues of 10–14 teams. The standard full-featured format.
  Seeds 1 and 2 receive a first-round bye.

  WEEK 1 (Wild Card / First Round):
    Winners Bracket:  Seed 3 vs Seed 6 | Seed 4 vs Seed 5
    Losers Bracket:   (no losers bracket games this week — only 4 teams playing)

  WEEK 2 (Semifinals):
    Winners Bracket Semifinals:
      Seed 1 vs lowest remaining seed | Seed 2 vs other remaining seed
    Losers Bracket (Consolation Semifinals):
      Wild Card losers from Week 1 play each other to stay alive for
      the 5th/6th place game

  WEEK 3 (Finals):
    Winners Bracket Championship: determines 1st and 2nd place
    Winners Bracket 3rd Place Game: semifinal losers play for 3rd place
    Losers Bracket 5th Place Game: consolation semifinal winners play for 5th
    6th Place: consolation semifinal loser — last place in playoffs

  Final standings for all 6 playoff teams are officially recorded.
  Seeds 7+ finish at their regular season standing.

FORMAT D — FULL LEAGUE PLAYOFFS (3 playoff weeks)
  For leagues of 8–12 teams where the commissioner wants everyone in the
  postseason. All teams are seeded and placed into either the winners or
  losers bracket from the start.

  In a 10-team league:
  Seeds 1–4 enter the Winners Bracket
  Seeds 5–10 enter the Losers Bracket (or a separate consolation bracket)

  WINNERS BRACKET follows the same structure as Format C for seeds 1–4.

  LOSERS / CONSOLATION BRACKET:
    Week 1: Seed 5 vs Seed 10 | Seed 6 vs Seed 9 | Seed 7 vs Seed 8
    Week 2: Winners advance; losers are eliminated (finish at their seed)
    Week 3: Consolation championship determines 5th place overall

  This format ensures every team plays meaningful games in the final weeks.

--- 8.4 Seeding & Tiebreakers for Playoff Entry ---

At the end of the regular season, teams are seeded by:
  1. Win/loss record (most wins = highest seed)
  2. Head-to-head record (if two teams have identical records)
  3. Total correct slate picks (season aggregate) — points for
  4. Total opponent correct slate picks (season aggregate) — points against
  5. Random coin flip (server-side, seeded and logged for auditing)

Tiebreakers are applied in order. Once a tiebreaker separates two teams, the
remaining tiebreakers are not evaluated for that pairing.

--- 8.5 Playoff Matchup Mechanics ---

Playoff weeks work identically to regular season weeks in terms of gameplay:
  - The weekly draft occurs on the same scheduled day/time as the regular season
  - All draft rules, minimum odds thresholds, and slate rules apply unchanged
  - Head-to-head scoring is identical (most correct starting slate wins)
  - The same tiebreaker rules apply for individual matchup ties

There are no special rules or power-ups in the playoffs. The same skill set
that earned a team their playoff seed is what determines the champion.

--- 8.6 Weeks Per Playoff Round ---

Each playoff round is one week (one full NFL game slate). The number of playoff
weeks consumed by the chosen format is:

  Format A: 2 playoff weeks
  Format B: 2 playoff weeks
  Format C: 3 playoff weeks
  Format D: 3 playoff weeks

The commissioner must ensure the regular season length + playoff weeks does not
exceed the available NFL weeks in the season. The platform enforces this at
league creation and warns the commissioner if the configuration would run past
the NFL regular season calendar.

--- 8.7 Champion Determination & Payout Trigger ---

The winner of the Winners Bracket championship game is the league champion.
When the final playoff week's results are processed, the platform automatically
transitions the league to COMPLETED state and triggers the PAYOUT_JOB. Official
finish positions (1st, 2nd, 3rd, etc.) are recorded permanently on each user's
career profile.

9. MONEY, PAYOUTS & PLATFORM REVENUE
================================================================================

--- 9.1 The League Pot ---

When a user joins a league and pays their buy-in, the funds are collected by
the platform via Stripe and held in trust. The total pot = buy-in amount ×
number of league members.

Example: 10-team league, $20 buy-in = $200 total pot.

--- 9.2 Platform Commission ---

The platform takes 10% of the total pot. This fee is non-negotiable and applies
to all leagues.

Example: $200 pot × 10% = $20 platform fee. $180 is distributed to winners.

--- 9.3 Payout Distribution ---

The commissioner configures how the remaining 90% is distributed. Options:

  - 100% to 1st place (winner takes all)
  - 90% to 1st / 10% to 2nd
  - 80% to 1st / 15% to 2nd / 5% to 3rd
  - Custom splits (commissioner inputs percentages that must sum to 100%)

The payout configuration is set at league creation and locked once the season
begins. The platform enforces that the sum of all payout percentages equals
exactly 100% of the distributable amount (i.e., 90% of the total pot).

--- 9.4 Payout Processing ---

When the champion is determined (see Section 8.7), the platform automatically initiates payouts
via Stripe. Winners receive their payout to their registered payout method
(bank account, debit card) within 3–5 business days, consistent with standard
Stripe payout timelines.

The platform's 10% commission is retained in the platform's Stripe account.

All payout transactions are logged for financial auditing and tax reporting
compliance. Users who receive $600 or more in combined payouts in a calendar
year will be issued a 1099 form per IRS requirements.

--- 9.5 Refunds & Edge Cases ---

  - League cancelled before season start: full refunds to all members
  - User who leaves before season starts: full refund (commissioner must approve
    or a configurable grace period applies — e.g., 48 hours before draft)
  - User who leaves after season starts: no refund (their team remains in the
    league as a "ghost" team managed entirely by auto-pick for the remainder)
  - If a season cannot be completed due to an NFL cancellation or force majeure:
    refunds are prorated based on weeks completed vs. total season weeks, minus
    the platform's pro-rated commission for weeks played

11. ODDS INTEGRATION (THE-ODDS-API.COM)
================================================================================

--- 11.1 Why the-odds-api.com ---

The-odds-api.com is a cost-effective, developer-friendly odds data provider
that offers American odds across major sports including NFL. It is well-suited
for this application because:

  - Our use case does NOT require tick-by-tick real-time odds (unlike a live
    sportsbook). We need odds at two specific moments:
      1. Draft pool creation (24–48 hours before draft)
      2. Ongoing slate management display (updated every few hours)
  - The API has a clear, documented rate limit structure with quota-based
    billing, making costs predictable and manageable
  - It supports player props (which are the core of our slate picks)
    as well as game lines (spread, moneyline, totals)

--- 11.2 Data We Pull ---

From the-odds-api.com, we retrieve:
  - NFL game schedules (teams, dates, times)
  - Player prop markets (receiving yards, receptions, passing yards, etc.)
  - Game outcome markets (moneyline, spread, totals)
  - Odds in American format from multiple bookmakers (we use a consensus or
    single primary bookmaker — configurable)

--- 11.3 When We Pull Data ---

DRAFT POOL CREATION (weekly, mid-week):
  - A scheduled background job fires mid-week (e.g., Wednesday morning)
  - Pulls all available NFL player props and game lines for the upcoming Sunday
    and Monday games
  - Filters picks by eligibility (odds at or above the minimum threshold)
  - Stores a snapshot of odds in our database — these are the "draft odds" and
    do not change for draft eligibility purposes

SLATE MANAGEMENT DISPLAY (ongoing, lower frequency):
  - A background job pulls updated odds periodically (e.g., every 2–3 hours
    during the week, more frequently on game day)
  - Updates the stored "current odds" field on each slate pick record
  - Rate limiting and quota management are carefully handled here (see Section 17)

RESULT FETCHING (post-game):
  - After games conclude, a job fetches final scores and stats to grade picks
  - This may use a separate data source for confirmed final stats (the-odds-api
    may not be the best source for final stat grading — a secondary sports data
    API may be used for result grading only, e.g., a free-tier stats API)

--- 11.4 Odds Display Philosophy ---

Odds shown to users during slate management are the best available current
odds as of the last API refresh. The platform clearly communicates the last-
updated timestamp to users so they understand they are not seeing live tick-
by-tick data. A "refresh" button allows users to manually trigger a re-fetch
(subject to per-user rate limiting to prevent abuse).

12. NOTIFICATIONS & REAL-TIME CONSIDERATIONS

--- 12.1 Push Notifications ---

The platform sends push notifications (mobile) and email notifications for:

  - Draft starting soon (24 hours, 1 hour, 15 minutes before)
  - It's your turn to pick (during the draft)

The draft screen requires near-real-time updates (other users' picks appearing

  - WebSocket or Server-Sent Events (SSE) connection maintained during the draft

  - A dedicated broadcast channel per league draft session
  - Mobile clients reconnect gracefully if the connection drops

Outside of draft windows, slate management does not require WebSockets. Standard
HTTP polling or manual page refresh is acceptable, with odds refresh triggered
explicitly by the user or by a periodic client-side poll (e.g., every 5 minutes
on the slate management page).

13. MOBILE & TABLET UX PHILOSOPHY
================================================================================

The platform is designed mobile-first. The primary use case is a user managing
their slate from their phone while watching Sunday afternoon games.

Key UX principles:

  THUMB-FRIENDLY INTERACTIONS: All primary actions (swap pick to starter, swap
  to bench, confirm slate) are reachable in the lower half of the screen on
  mobile. No critical actions are buried in header menus.

  DRAFT SCREEN ON MOBILE: The draft is the most complex screen. On mobile, it
  uses a bottom-sheet pattern for the slate pick detail/confirmation, with the
  available slate picks list as the primary scrollable content. The countdown timer
  is always visible (sticky header or floating element).

  FAST LOAD: The app is a Vue 3 SPA. Route-based code splitting ensures the
  draft screen and slate management screen load quickly even on slower mobile
  connections. Critical data is pre-fetched on route entry.

  OFFLINE GRACEFUL DEGRADATION: If a user loses connectivity during the draft,
  the app shows a clear "reconnecting" state and does not allow picks to be
  submitted until the connection is confirmed restored.

  TABLET LAYOUT: On tablet-sized screens, the draft and slate management screens use a
  split-panel layout: picks list on the left, current roster on the right.
  This mirrors the desktop multi-column experience.

  DARK MODE: The app supports system-level dark/light mode switching. All
  color tokens are designed to work in both modes.

================================================================================
16. BACKEND ARCHITECTURE & JOB QUEUE PHILOSOPHY
================================================================================

--- 16.1 Core Philosophy ---

All heavy, slow, or batch-processing operations are handled as background jobs
dispatched to Laravel's queue system. This keeps HTTP request/response cycles
fast and ensures the app remains responsive under load.

The queue driver starts as the database driver (Laravel's built-in, using MySQL)
for simplicity in MVP. The job structure is built so that migrating to Redis
(and later Kubernetes-based workers) requires only configuration changes, not
architectural rewrites.

--- 16.2 Identified Background Jobs ---

SLATE_POOL_BUILD_JOB
  Trigger: Laravel Scheduler, fires automatically 15–30 minutes before each
           league's scheduled weekly draft start time. Since different leagues
           may have different draft days/times (commissioner-configurable),
           this job is dispatched per-league based on each league's stored
           draft schedule, not on a single global cron. A global scheduler
           check runs every 5 minutes and dispatches SLATE_POOL_BUILD_JOB
           for any league whose draft starts within the next 30 minutes and
           whose pool has not yet been built for the current week.
  Purpose: Fetch the latest NFL odds from the-odds-api.com at the freshest
           possible moment before the draft begins; filter eligible picks
           (above minimum odds threshold, games far enough from kickoff);
           create the slate pool records for the league's upcoming draft.
  Notes:   Rate-limit aware; a single API fetch is made and reused across all
           leagues drafting within the same window (within a configurable
           deduplication interval, e.g., leagues drafting within 30 minutes
           of each other on the same day share one API fetch). The draft
           lobby is held in "preparing" state until this job completes.

ODDS_REFRESH_JOB
  Trigger: Laravel Scheduler, every 2–3 hours during active weeks
  Purpose: Update "current odds" on all active picks for slate management
           display purposes
  Notes:   Batched API calls; respects quota limits; throttled per Section 16

DRAFT_AUTOPICK_JOB
  Trigger: Draft timer expiry event (dispatched by the draft WebSocket handler)
  Purpose: When a user's pick timer expires, auto-select the highest-odds
           eligible remaining pick in the pool on their behalf
  Notes:   Must process within seconds; high priority queue

DRAFT_ADVANCE_JOB
  Trigger: After each slate pick is confirmed (manual or auto)
  Purpose: Advance the draft to the next user in the snake order, start their
           countdown timer, broadcast the state change to all connected clients

SLATE_LOCK_JOB
  Trigger: Laravel Scheduler, checked every 5 minutes; also triggered by
           game start time events
  Purpose: Mark all picks for a game as "locked" once kickoff time passes;
           prevents any further slate swaps for those picks

RESULT_GRADING_JOB
  Trigger: Laravel Scheduler, post-game windows (e.g., Monday 12:00 AM,
           Tuesday 6:00 AM for cleanup)
  Purpose: Fetch final game stats, grade each slate pick (hit/miss/void), update
           pick outcome records

MATCHUP_SCORE_JOB
  Trigger: Dispatched by RESULT_GRADING_JOB upon completion of grading for
           a given week's full game slate
  Purpose: Calculate each team's weekly score (count of correct starting slate),
           determine matchup winners, update win/loss records

STANDINGS_UPDATE_JOB
  Trigger: Dispatched by MATCHUP_SCORE_JOB
  Purpose: Recalculate and update league standings, handle tiebreakers

PAYOUT_JOB
  Trigger: Manually triggered by platform admin or auto-triggered when season
           state transitions to COMPLETED
  Purpose: Calculate final payouts per the league's configured structure,
           initiate Stripe transfers to winners, record all transactions

NOTIFICATION_DISPATCH_JOB
  Trigger: Various (draft start, pick result, payout, etc.)
  Purpose: Send push notifications and emails to relevant users
  Notes:   Dispatched from other jobs; separated to prevent notification
           failures from blocking core game logic

REFUND_JOB
  Trigger: League cancellation event
  Purpose: Issue Stripe refunds to all buy-in payers for a cancelled league

--- 16.3 Queue Priority Levels ---

High priority queue:   DRAFT_AUTOPICK_JOB, DRAFT_ADVANCE_JOB
                       (time-sensitive; must process within seconds)

Default priority queue: RESULT_GRADING_JOB, MATCHUP_SCORE_JOB,
                        STANDINGS_UPDATE_JOB, SLATE_LOCK_JOB

Low priority queue:    ODDS_REFRESH_JOB, SLATE_POOL_BUILD_JOB,
                       NOTIFICATION_DISPATCH_JOB, PAYOUT_JOB, REFUND_JOB

In the MVP (database driver), these are represented as separate named queues.
In a Kubernetes deployment, each queue maps to its own worker deployment with
independently scalable replica counts.

--- 16.4 Job Retry & Failure Handling ---

All jobs define:
  - Maximum retry attempts (e.g., 3 retries with exponential backoff)
  - A failed job handler that logs the failure and notifies the admin
  - Idempotency keys where applicable (e.g., PAYOUT_JOB checks if a payout
    record already exists before initiating a Stripe transfer)

A dead-letter queue (failed_jobs table in Laravel) captures all permanently
failed jobs for admin review.

--- 16.6 Laravel Horizon ---

Laravel Horizon provides the real-time dashboard and management interface for
all queue activity on the platform. It is the primary tool for monitoring job
health, diagnosing failures, and tuning worker throughput.

Horizon is configured via config/horizon.php and runs as a long-lived process
managed by Supervisor (or the equivalent in the deployment environment). It
requires Redis as the queue driver — this is one of the key reasons the
architecture migrates from the database queue driver (MVP) to Redis (production)
as a prerequisite for Horizon being fully operational.

WHAT HORIZON PROVIDES:

  Real-time dashboard: Live view of jobs processing, queued, completed, and
  failed across all named queues (high, default, low priority). Throughput
  and wait time metrics are visible per queue.

  Job history and search: Full searchable history of completed and failed jobs,
  including payload inspection. Essential for debugging result grading jobs,
  payout jobs, and slate pool build jobs where the input data matters.

  Failed job management: Failed jobs are surfaced in the Horizon UI with their
  exception details and full payload. Admins can retry individual failed jobs
  or bulk-retry by queue directly from the dashboard without needing artisan
  commands.

  Queue metrics and throughput graphs: Historical throughput per queue over
  time, allowing the team to identify bottlenecks (e.g., Sunday night spikes
  when RESULT_GRADING_JOB and MATCHUP_SCORE_JOB fire across many leagues
  simultaneously).

  Worker auto-balancing: Horizon's balancing strategies (simple, auto) can
  be configured to dynamically redistribute worker processes across queues
  based on queue depth — ensuring the high-priority draft queue always has
  available workers during peak draft windows.

HORIZON ACCESS:
  The Horizon dashboard (/horizon) is restricted to platform admin users via
  Laravel's Horizon gate, defined in the HorizonServiceProvider. It is never
  accessible to regular league users or commissioners.

HORIZON IN MVP vs. PRODUCTION:
  Horizon is included in the MVP. Redis is required for Horizon to be fully
  operational and is straightforward to run locally at no cost:

    macOS:   brew install redis && brew services start redis
    Linux:   apt install redis-server
    Windows: WSL2 (then apt install redis-server), or Laragon (bundles Redis
             out of the box), or Laravel Herd Pro (one-click Redis toggle)

  In all cases, set REDIS_HOST=127.0.0.1 in the local .env file. No Docker
  or additional infrastructure is needed for local development. Redis runs
  as a lightweight background service on the default port 6379.

  The queue driver should be set to Redis (QUEUE_CONNECTION=redis) from the
  start of development — there is no reason to begin with the database driver
  given how simple the local Redis setup is. This keeps the local environment
  consistent with production and means Horizon's dashboard is fully functional
  from day one of development.

--- 16.7 Kubernetes Migration Path ---

When the platform scales to warrant Kubernetes:
  - Switch queue driver from database to Redis (change in .env only)
  - Deploy Redis as a managed service or in-cluster
  - Each named queue becomes a Kubernetes Deployment with its own worker pods
  - High-priority queues get more replicas and guaranteed resource allocation
  - Horizontal Pod Autoscaler scales worker pods based on queue depth
  - No application code changes required — only infrastructure configuration

This migration path is the explicit reason for building with named queues and
strict job separation from day one.

17. API RATE LIMITING & THROTTLING STRATEGY
================================================================================

--- 17.1 the-odds-api.com Quota Management ---

The-odds-api.com uses a request quota model (not rate-per-second). Each API
call consumes a certain number of quota units depending on the endpoint and
number of markets requested.

Our strategy to minimize quota consumption:

  a) FETCH ONCE, FAN OUT: When building the slate pool, we make a single set of
     API calls to fetch all available NFL props for the week. The results are
     stored in our database. All leagues use this single cached dataset — we
     do not make one API call per league.

  b) SCHEDULED REFRESHES ONLY: We never make on-demand API calls in response to
     individual user requests (except the user-triggered "refresh odds" button,
     which has its own per-user rate limit). All data fetching happens via
     scheduled background jobs.

  c) SELECTIVE MARKET FETCHING: We only request the specific prop markets we
     support (e.g., player receptions, passing yards, rushing yards, game
     moneyline). We do not pull all available markets — this reduces quota usage
     per call.

  d) CONFIGURABLE REFRESH FREQUENCY: The odds refresh interval is stored as a
     platform configuration value (not hardcoded). When upgrading to a higher
     API plan with more quota, the admin can increase the refresh frequency via
     a config change without a code deployment.

--- 17.2 Platform-Level API Rate Limiting ---

In addition to managing our outbound API usage, we rate limit inbound requests
to our own API to prevent abuse:

  - User-triggered "refresh odds" button: max 1 request per user per 10 minutes
  - Draft pick submission: no special rate limit (time-constrained by design)
  - Lineup swap: max 20 swaps per user per hour (prevents scripted manipulation)
  - General API: Laravel's built-in throttle middleware (60 requests/minute
    per authenticated user for most endpoints)

--- 17.3 Configurable Limits ---

All rate limit values are stored in the Laravel config system (not hardcoded).
This means they can be adjusted via environment variables or a platform admin
setting without code changes:

  - ODDS_API_REFRESH_INTERVAL_HOURS (default: 3)
  - ODDS_API_SLATE_POOL_BUILD_DAY (default: Wednesday)
  - USER_ODDS_REFRESH_COOLDOWN_MINUTES (default: 10)
  - USER_LINEUP_SWAP_LIMIT_PER_HOUR (default: 20)
  - API_GENERAL_THROTTLE_PER_MINUTE (default: 60)

18. DATA MODELS (CONCEPTUAL, PLAIN ENGLISH)
================================================================================

This section describes the major entities in plain English. Full schema design
with migrations will be produced in the technical .md document.

USERS
  Core identity: id, email, password hash, display name, avatar, timestamps.
  Linked to: subscriptions, team memberships, payout methods.

LEAGUES
  Represents a single competition. Fields include: name, type (public/private),
  state (pending/active/playoffs/completed/cancelled), commissioner (user_id),
  all configuration settings (buy-in, payout structure, slate size, min odds,
  draft settings, season length), current week, invite code (for private
  leagues), total pot amount collected.

LEAGUE_MEMBERSHIPS
  Junction table between users and leagues. Includes: team name, team logo,
  current record (wins/losses/ties), season stats. One record per user per
  league.

SEASONS
  Tracks the active season for a league. Includes week number tracking,
  start/end dates, playoff structure.

WEEKLY_MATCHUPS
  One record per pair of teams matched in a given week. Includes: week number,
  home/away team (arbitrary labeling), scores, and winner.

SLATE_POOLS
  One pool per league per week. Contains the set of available slate picks for that
  week's draft. Includes metadata about when it was built and the snapshot
  odds used.

PICK_SELECTIONS (the core entity — the "players" of this game)
  Represents one available pick in a slate pool. Fields include: description,
  game reference, game time (kickoff), pick type (player prop, game line),
  sport, snapshot odds (at pool creation), current odds (updated periodically),
  outcome (pending / hit / miss / void).

SLATE_PICKS
  Represents a slate pick that has been drafted by a specific team. Junction of
  league_membership + pick_selection + week. Includes: slot assignment
  (starter slot 1–5 or bench slot 1–3), lock status, slate swap history
  (for auditing), drafted_odds (the snapshot odds at the moment this pick
  was selected during the draft), and locked_odds (the odds at the moment
  this pick's game kicked off and the slate pick permanently locked into its
  current starter or bench position). The delta between drafted_odds and
  locked_odds is the "odds drift" for that pick — a positive drift means
  the slate pick became a bigger favorite after the draft (the user captured
  value by selecting it early), a negative drift means it became a longer
  shot (the market moved against the slate pick's implied probability after the
  draft). Odds drift is stored on every slate pick record regardless of whether
  it started or sat on the bench, enabling full historical analysis at the
  pick, week, user, and league level.

TRANSACTIONS
  Financial records for all buy-ins, payouts, and platform commissions. Includes:
  user_id, league_id, amount, type (buy_in / payout / commission / refund),
  stripe_reference, status, timestamps.

NOTIFICATIONS
  Log of all notifications sent (user, type, content, delivered_at).

PLATFORM_SETTINGS
  Key-value store for configurable platform parameters (API limits, feature
  flags, default league settings).

JOB_LOG (optional but recommended)
  Log of background job executions for auditing: job type, league/week context,
  started_at, completed_at, status, notes. Useful for debugging slate pool builds
  and result grading.

19. USER CAREER STATS & PROFILE ANALYTICS
================================================================================

Every user accumulates a persistent career profile that spans all leagues and
all seasons they have ever participated in. This profile is visible on their
public user page and serves as both a bragging-rights display and a meaningful
measure of long-term skill. All stats below are tracked globally (across all
leagues) as well as per-league and per-season where relevant.

--- 18.1 Record & Finish History ---

  Overall win/loss/tie record (all matchups, all leagues, all seasons)
  Number of league championships (1st place finishes)
  Number of 2nd place finishes
  Number of 3rd place finishes
  Number of seasons participated in
  Number of unique leagues played in
  Total playoff appearances

--- 18.2 Pick Accuracy Stats ---

  Overall pick hit rate: correct slate picks / total starting slate (all time)
  Weekly high score: personal best correct slate picks in a single week
  Longest matchup win streak (consecutive weekly head-to-head wins)
  Longest correct pick streak (consecutive individual picks that hit)
  Hit rate by pick type (player prop vs. game line)
  Hit rate by pick category (receiving, rushing, passing, game outcome, etc.)
  Hit rate by NFL team (e.g., the user tends to nail Chiefs props)
  Hit rate by game window (Sunday 1pm, Sunday late, Monday Night, etc.)

--- 18.3 Odds Drift Stats (Signature Platform Metric) ---

Odds drift is the platform's signature skill metric and is unique to this
product. It measures the difference between the odds of a slate pick at the moment
a user selected it in the draft versus the odds at the moment that pick's game
kicked off and locked. It captures a user's ability to identify value in the
slate pool before the broader market corrects — essentially, how skilled a
user is at "buying low" during the draft.

How it works in the data:
  drafted_odds: American odds stored at the moment the user selected this pick
  locked_odds:  American odds fetched and stored at the moment of game kickoff
                (captured by the SLATE_LOCK_JOB when it locks each slate pick)

Both values are stored on every ROSTER_PICK record regardless of whether the
pick was in the starting slate or on the bench. This enables complete
historical analysis at every level.

Derived metrics tracked on the user's global career profile:

  AVERAGE ODDS DRIFT (starters): The average change in implied probability
  between drafted_odds and locked_odds across all picks the user has ever
  started. A consistently positive drift — meaning picks shortened in odds
  (became bigger favorites) after the user took them — suggests the user is
  a skilled drafter who identifies value before the market does. A negative
  drift suggests their slate tend to lengthen after the draft, meaning they
  may be systematically overvaluing certain picks or the market is moving
  against their reads.

  AVERAGE ODDS DRIFT (all picks): Same calculation but including bench slate,
  giving a full picture of draft quality independent of slate management decisions.

  BEST SINGLE ODDS DRIFT: The largest favorable odds movement on any single
  pick in the user's history. For example: drafted at +200, locked at -150
  — a massive swing in implied probability in the user's favor. Displayed as
  a career highlight on the profile.

  WORST SINGLE ODDS DRIFT: The single worst move in the user's history —
  a cautionary tale (e.g., took something at -180 that opened up to +220).

  ODDS DRIFT vs. OUTCOME CORRELATION: Whether the user's starts with favorable
  drift (picks that shortened) actually hit at a higher rate than their slate
  with unfavorable drift. A strong positive correlation is the mark of a
  genuinely skilled market reader — they're not just getting lucky when the
  odds move their way. This is an advanced stat displayed as a secondary
  insight on the profile.

This cluster of stats is surfaced to users as a "Market IQ" or "Draft Value"
rating — a number that tells a user how well they're reading the slate pool
relative to where the market ultimately lands. It is one of the features that
most differentiates DraftSlate from any existing slate or fantasy product.

--- 18.4 Financial History (Private — User-Visible Only) ---

  Total buy-ins paid (career)
  Total winnings received (career)
  Net return (winnings minus buy-ins, all time)
  ROI percentage

This data is strictly private — visible only to the authenticated user on
their own account dashboard. It is never displayed publicly.

--- 18.5 Public Profile Display ---

The public-facing user profile shows (with user-configurable visibility per
stat category):
  - Display name and avatar
  - Overall win/loss/tie record
  - Championship badges (one distinct badge per title won)
  - 2nd and 3rd place finish badges
  - Career pick hit rate
  - Average odds drift (starters) and Draft Value rating
  - Longest correct pick streak
  - Number of seasons / leagues played
  - Currently active leagues (optional — user can hide)

20. KEY BUSINESS RULES & CONSTRAINTS
================================================================================

This section consolidates critical rules that must be enforced by the system:

  1.  A league must have an even number of teams (required for head-to-head
      matchup pairing). Odd team counts are prevented at creation.

  2.  A user cannot join a league after the season has started (Week 1 draft
      has opened or concluded).

  3.  Commissioner cannot change buy-in amount, payout structure, or roster
      size after the season has begun.

  4.  Payout percentages configured by the commissioner must sum to exactly 100%.

  5.  Each pick in the slate pool can only be drafted by one team per week.

  6.  Odds enforcement is applied at the time of slate pool snapshot creation
      (when the SLATE_POOL_BUILD_JOB runs), not at the moment of each user's
      individual pick selection during the draft. In Global Floor mode, each
      pick must independently meet the floor — no averaging or aggregate
      loophole. In Per-Slot Bands mode, each slate pick must fall within the odds
      band defined for the slot it is being drafted into; server-side
      validation rejects any slate pick/slot combination that violates the band.

  7.  Pick locks are enforced server-side at game kickoff time. Client-side
      timer display is informational only.

  8.  Starting slate slots lock independently, on a per-pick basis, at the
      kickoff time of that pick's specific game — not at a universal weekly
      lock time. This mirrors how fantasy sports handle individual player
      lock times. A user may swap a Sunday 4:25pm pick in or out of their
      starting slate right up until that game kicks off, even if their
      Sunday 1:00pm picks have already locked. If a starting slot is still
      occupied by an unlocked bench pick when that pick's game kicks off, it
      locks in its current position (starter or bench) at that moment. There
      is no system auto-fill — if a user neglects to fill a starting slot and
      a game kicks off with that slot vacant (because the slate pick assigned there
      was on the bench and they never moved it), that slot simply scores 0
      for the week. Managing the timing of swaps relative to game times is
      an intentional and valued skill layer of the game.

  9.  Auto-pick during draft selects the highest-odds eligible pick above the
      minimum threshold that has not yet been taken. Ties in odds broken by
      earliest game time (to maximize the slate pick's "time to play").

  10. A team that forfeits (user account deactivated mid-season) remains in
      the league on auto-pilot — all future drafts use auto-pick, and their
      bye weeks in matchups give the opponent a free win.

  11. The platform's 10% commission is taken from the gross pot. The
      commissioner cannot reduce this percentage or waive it.

  12. Refunds via Stripe are initiated within 24 hours of a league cancellation.

  13. All financial transactions are immutable once confirmed. Corrections
      require a separate compensating transaction, not an edit to the original.

  14. The draft auto-advance happens only when the previous pick (manual or
      auto) is fully committed to the database before the next user's timer
      starts. Race conditions in draft state are resolved via database-level
      locking or optimistic concurrency.

  15. Users are limited to joining a reasonable number of leagues simultaneously
      (configurable — default: 10) to prevent farming/abuse.

21. FUTURE CONSIDERATIONS (OUT OF MVP SCOPE)
================================================================================

These features are explicitly excluded from the MVP but should be kept in mind
when designing data models and architecture to avoid painting ourselves into
a corner:

  MULTI-SPORT SUPPORT
  Extending beyond NFL to NBA, MLB, NHL, college football. The pick_selections
  and draft_pools tables should include a sport/league field from day one (even
  if only NFL values are used in MVP).

  ADVANCED STATS & PICK ANALYTICS
  Per-user pick accuracy trends, best/worst categories, head-to-head history,
  historical pick performance by player/team. Premium subscription feature.

  BRAND-SPONSORED CHALLENGES
  A weekly featured "challenge pick" sponsored by a brand — e.g., "Gatorade
  player of the week over-under." Revenue share with brand partners.

  COMMISSIONER TOOLS EXPANSION
  Draft order customization, trade windows (swap drafted slate picks between teams
  before lock), commissioner veto powers.

  SOCIAL FEATURES
  League chat/message board, pick commentary (users can annotate why they made
  a slate pick), public pick sharing to social media.

  NATIVE MOBILE APPS
  MVP ships as a mobile-optimized PWA. Native iOS and Android apps (wrapping
  the Vue frontend via Capacitor, or fully native) are a post-MVP milestone.

  PICK RESULT SOURCE REDUNDANCY
  For grading picks, the-odds-api.com may not be the best source for confirmed
  final player stats. A secondary sports stats API (e.g., a free-tier sports
  reference API) should be integrated for result grading to ensure accuracy and
  reduce dependency on a single vendor.

  RESPONSIBLE GAMBLING FEATURES
  Deposit limits, self-exclusion tools, and responsible gambling messaging.
  Required in some jurisdictions if the platform ever expands into regulated
  territory.

  LEGAL COMPLIANCE REVIEW
  Before launch, a legal review of the "fantasy sports" vs. "gambling" question
  under applicable state laws is essential. The free-to-play vs. real-money
  distinction, the skill vs. chance argument, and the draft mechanism all
  factor into this. This is a business-critical pre-launch task, not a
  post-launch afterthought.
